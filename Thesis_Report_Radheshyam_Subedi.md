## Abstract
Algorithmic portfolio management heavily relies on dynamic price covariance, rendering automated systems uniquely vulnerable to tail-risk and systemic market shocks. Traditional models, such as Markowitz's Mean-Variance Optimization (MVO), as well as modern Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) networks, suffer from the "1/N trap," diverging into uniform allocations when confronted with non-stationary financial data. Furthermore, these frameworks are entirely "price-blind," ignoring the fundamental economic realities and overlapping vulnerabilities of corporate operations. This thesis proposes a novel Hierarchical Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) framework that explicitly enforces structural diversification. By integrating Natural Language Processing (NLP) to parse SEC EDGAR 10-K business descriptions, the system calculates a mathematically rigorous "Weight-Aware Lexical Penalty," penalizing holding assets with highly correlated fundamental operations. Additionally, the architecture abandons the standard Softmax output layer, substituting it with a Dirichlet-based policy to allow Manager agents to execute high-conviction, non-uniform capital shifts. Tested via Walk-Forward Validation across the 2020 COVID-19 crash and the 2022 inflationary bear market, the Staged Hierarchical MARL system dramatically outperformed classical baselines. On unseen holdout data (2022-2023), the model achieved an 8.86% return with a Sharpe ratio of 0.577, substantially defeating the Equal-Weight (Sharpe: 0.312) and MVO (Sharpe: 0.362) baselines, while effectively compartmentalizing systemic risk and enhancing drawdown recovery. This comprehensive study validates that substituting statistical pricing variance with semantic business isolation provides a quantifiable mechanism for algorithmic resilience.

## Chapter 1 Introduction 

### 1.1 Background
Managing risk in the stock market is essentially a computational battle against non-stationarity and uncertainty. The financial markets are not static environments governed by fixed physical laws; rather, they are highly dynamic, emotionally driven, and structurally complex ecosystems where the rules of engagement constantly shift. While the foundational Modern Portfolio Theory suggests that spreading capital across assets with low historical covariance reduces portfolio variance (Markowitz, 1952), the reality of algorithmic trading proves that price-based diversification is highly fragile. Markowitz's brilliant postulation assumed that rational investors exclusively seek to optimize the mean-variance frontier, minimizing standard deviation for a given target return. In a mathematically perfect world, if Asset A and Asset B possess a correlation coefficient of zero, an equal capital distribution between them mathematically isolates the portfolio from idiosyncratic shocks.

However, historical covariance is an illusion heavily dependent on the sampling window. During systemic macro-economic shocks, such as the 2020 COVID-19 crash, historical correlation matrices break down entirely. Assets that previously appeared statistically independent suddenly exhibit massive tail dependence, moving downward in lockstep as market liquidity evaporates (Flint, Seymour and Chikurunhe, 2020). When margin calls cascade across the institutional landscape, investors sell whatever is liquid, entirely regardless of underlying asset class or historical correlation. During these "black swan" events, the mathematical foundations of mean-variance optimization violently disintegrate, leaving automated systems entirely exposed to catastrophic drawdowns.

This research is motivated by the engineering reality that historical Open, High, Low, Close, Volume (OHLCV) price data alone lacks the dimensionality required to capture a company's true economic exposure. Machine Learning architectures explicitly optimized on pure OHLCV tensors are functionally "blind" to the real world. They ingest numerical representations of past human sentiment but fail to interpret the physical constraints and liabilities defining corporate operability. Holding twenty different technology equities might appear diversified mathematically when observing a calm, bullish three-year sampling window, but structurally, it represents a catastrophic concentration of supply-chain reliance and interest-rate vulnerability. If a localized macroeconomic shock destabilizes trans-Pacific semiconductor supply lines, the entire algorithmic portfolio suffers simultaneous liquidation. The AI's internal calculus?which assumed safety derived from historical price variances?proves fatal because the AI fundamentally did not "know" what it was buying.

By applying Big Data techniques and Natural Language Processing (NLP) to parse official SEC EDGAR 10-K filings, algorithms can transcend price and map the actual business activities, operational ecosystems, and material liabilities of companies (Loughran and McDonald, 2011). The 10-K filing, specifically 'Item 1: Business Description', constitutes a legally binding, intensely audited disclosure outlining the fundamental revenue mechanics of an organization. Utilizing NLP to convert these disclosures into vectorized semantic constraints gives the optimizing agent an essential layer of "structural awareness." Under the guidance of Prof. Saeed Sharif and Dr. Yara, this study develops a Hierarchical Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) "Manager-Worker" architecture that actively avoids holding companies with overlapping semantic profiles, enforcing true structural diversification over mere statistical illusion.

### 1.2 Problem Statement
The central problem in continuous-action portfolio management is that highly complex, computationally expensive Artificial Intelligence models frequently fail to outperform a naive, equal-weight (1/N) baseline out-of-sample. This systemic failure represents a profound embarrassment for the quantitative finance community and is widely known as "The 1/N Trap." Because financial data is inherently noisy and intensely non-stationary, Reinforcement Learning optimizers inevitably face a massive signal-to-noise deficit. During the gradient descent process, when the agent observes contradictory rewards stemming from identical state observations (an inherent feature of stochastic market responses), the optimizer defaults to spreading capital uniformly across the asset universe as a mathematical defense mechanism (DeMiguel, Garlappi and Uppal, 2009). The AI learns that doing nothing?distributing capital equally?is computationally safer than attempting to isolate transient alpha, rendering the millions of parameters within the deep neural network entirely redundant.

A major technical flaw underlying this behavior in standard RL agents is their reliance on the Softmax activation layer in the final action space. Because Softmax mathematically forces outputs to sum to one via exponential normalization, it naturally compresses the weights toward the center of the continuous distribution envelope. The Softmax function is inherently centripetal. It requires an exponentially massive pre-activation logit to output a sparse, high-conviction decision (e.g., assigning a 95% weight to a single high-conviction asset and 0% to others). The gradient required to force the Softmax layer towards the edges of the probability simplex vanishes exponentially, making it almost impossible for the network to output a 0% or 95% allocation for any single asset. Consequently, the agent becomes permanently trapped in a flat, uniform distribution and loses the ability to react aggressively when market regimes change (Tian et al., 2022). 

When you combine this devastating "Softmax Bias" with a complete blindness to actual business operations (due to exclusive reliance on OHLCV arrays), the resulting algorithmic behavior is acutely predictable and inherently dangerous. The agents will herd into statistically similar equities that temporarily exhibit low volatility, creating a "false safety" configuration. This leaves the entire portfolio structurally exposed to singular macro-economic crashes, as the model lacks the semantic awareness to recognize that it has densely aggregated capital into assets governed by parallel operational risks.

To solve this intertwined mechanical and structural crisis, this research completely removes the Softmax layer from the policy network. Instead, it implements a Hierarchical MARL system utilizing a Dirichlet-based continuous policy framework. The Dirichlet parameterization fundamentally allows the Manager agent to generate multi-modal, highly skewed probability distributions, enabling rapid, high-conviction concentrated allocations when systemic risks necessitate massive portfolio rotations (Xue and Ye, 2025). This critical architectural shift is then explicitly paired with a bespoke Weight-Aware Lexical Penalty, injecting NLP-derived structural intelligence directly into the agent's reward objective to strictly eliminate semantic redundancy and prevent catastrophic tail-event overlap.

#### 2.1.1 The Failure of Classical Optimization
The absolute traditional baseline for systematic portfolio allocation remains Harry Markowitz?s (1952) Mean-Variance Optimization (MVO). Markowitz revolutionized finance by arguing that investors could systematically achieve a metaphorical "free lunch" through mathematical diversification based strictly on the evaluation of historical covariance. Markowitz introduced the mathematical certainty that aggregating variance profiles correctly yields a combined portfolio variance lower than the sum of its individual parts. However, modern statistical researchers and quantitative engineers heavily debate the practical application of this framework. MVO provides mathematically perfect capital allocation exclusively in-sample, but its absolute reliance on historical data matrices causes it to fail violently out-of-sample when utilized without structural interpretation.

This inherent instability was overwhelmingly critiqued in a landmark paper by DeMiguel, Garlappi, and Uppal (2009). In their exhaustive evaluation of 14 separate advanced, complex statistical optimization models operating across diverse asset arrays, they proved astonishingly that the vast majority of highly complex mathematical models routinely fail to outpace a fundamentally naive 1/N (equal-weight) strategy operating in real-world scenarios. This widespread phenomenon, critically dubbed the "1/N Trap," highlights a deeply embedded fundamental inefficiency located within systems relying entirely on trailing price matrices. The core failure derives from estimation error; the covariance matrix computed from historical samples is an inherently flawed estimator of future asset behaviors regarding dynamic financial markets.

Furthermore, expanding the critique from simple estimation error toward systemic macroeconomic conditions, Flint, Seymour, and Chikurunhe (2020) demonstrated explicitly that classical statistical diversification entirely evaporates precisely when it is most needed: during systemic market shocks. Through massive quantitative evaluation, they proved that when panic liquidation occurs across institutional and retail markets simultaneously, localized correlation coefficients violently trend toward a universal 1.0. This phenomenon completely renders Markowitz's variance-reduction mechanics completely useless, validating that trailing statistical models possess no mechanism to absorb or defend against black-swan tail-risk dependence. 

In summary, the established financial and computational literature overwhelmingly indicates that traditional price-based optimization calculations are fundamentally broken during non-stationary regime shifts and rapid market crashes. This irrefutable structural failure fundamentally necessitates a radical paradigm shift away from static mathematical generalizations and forcefully toward flexible, dynamic, AI-driven contextual solutions capable of actively interpreting continuous state anomalies.

#### 2.1.2 Reinforcement Learning and Network Topologies
To strategically bypass the crippling estimation limitations anchoring static covariance matrices, the quantitative engineering field aggressively pivoted toward the implementation of model-free Reinforcement Learning. Jiang, Xu, and Liang (2017) established the seminal technical standard for DRL application in finance with the introduction of the Ensemble of Identical Independent Evaluators (EIIE) network topology and an integrated recurrent architecture titled Portfolio-Vector Memory (PVM). By intelligently utilizing One-Dimensional Convolutional (Conv1D) networks functioning with explicitly shared weights spanning identically across every distinct equity asset, the system successfully resolved the massive computational bottleneck of observing enormous multi-asset arrays simultaneously. Crucially, by enforcing generalized convolution layers rather than independent perceptrons, they successfully prevented the RL agent from simply memorizing specific out-performing stock tickers within the training sample, forcing the model to legitimately learn geometrical price morphologies.

However, complex empirical observations revealed that standard single-agent computational models continuously fail to stabilize when subjected to massively complex financial market environments characterized by extreme non-stationary reward signals. The highly structured, logical progression for deep learning researchers required moving directly toward Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) operations (Ning and Xie, 2024). Today, nevertheless, a massive structural and topological design debate exists prominently throughout the literature: should these massively parallel multi-agent systems be strictly arranged as "flat," concurrent execution grids or should they be vertically structured as "hierarchical" logic flows?

Many of the most prominent modern automated financial frameworks, exemplified primarily by the MAPS system developed by Lee et al., rely intrinsically on a "flat" execution architecture. In these configurations, fiercely independent algorithmic agents operate and dispatch trading commands completely simultaneously within a shared environment. Algorithms implementing frameworks resembling Multi-Agent Deep Deterministic Policy Gradients (MADDPG) (Lowe et al., 2017) attempt heroically to stabilize this highly competitive setup through centralized reward evaluation. In strict continuous financial applications, however, flat structures almost always inevitably generate catastrophic functional interference. Because the disparate agents fundamentally lack a central coordinating authority capable of macro-level decision logic, they consistently end up aggressively fighting each other to secure fractions of the identical overriding portfolio capital budget. This blind, concurrent scrambling effectively destroys the algorithmic stability and collapses the overall risk-adjusted return metric across out-of-sample data spans.

To efficiently and permanently solve this multi-agent instability paralysis, Xue and Ye (2025) computationally proved that engineering genuine structural resilience explicitly requires deep vertical hierarchical modeling. By categorically separating the governing macro-economic decisions (dictating precisely how much sweeping capital is functionally safe to risk) entirely from the granular micro-economic decisions (isolating specifically which exact individual corporate stock out of thousands to physically purchase), a rigid hierarchy systematically stops individual micro-agents from cannibalizing each other during reward accumulation cycles.

Single-agent models prove fatally narrow, while flat MARL execution configurations clearly suffer from continuous policy co-adaptation and deeply flawed internal budget competition. The expansive current literature proves overwhelmingly that designing a strict, two-tiered Manager-Worker hierarchy remains the singular most empirically stable methodology capable of achieving dynamic, exponentially scalable risk management protocols. This overwhelming technical rationale explicitly dictates why this precisely multi-level, cascading architecture was strategically selected as the cornerstone for this research structure.
